## 4. Executive Overview

The NAX30 ETF is a proposed regulated financial product intended to be structured as an exchange-traded fund (ETF) designed to track the performance of the [Namibia 30 Index (NAX30 Index)](https://nax30.io/).

The NAX30 Index is a rules-based equity benchmark referencing selected securities listed on the Namibia Securities Exchange (NSX). The Proposed Fund is intended to provide a regulated, institutionally governed, and exchange-listed access vehicle through which investors may obtain exposure to the performance of that benchmark.

The Proposed Fund is being contemplated as part of a broader benchmark infrastructure initiative in which the NAX30 Index functions as the core reference layer. Within this framework, the benchmark is the foundational reference instrument, while investable wrappers or market expressions may exist in separate forms and under separate market regimes.

The central premise of the Proposed Fund is that a rules-based Namibian equity benchmark may support the development of a formal ETF structure capable of serving institutional, professional, and benchmark-aware investors seeking a listed and regulated access mechanism to the underlying market.

## 5. Background and Market Rationale

Many developed and mature capital markets rely on benchmark indices that serve as reference measures for market performance, portfolio construction, asset allocation, and product development. Such indices often provide the basis for passive funds, exchange-traded products, structured notes, and other financial instruments.

In the Namibian context, the development of a rules-based benchmark-linked ETF has the potential to address a structural gap in listed investment products. While investors may access individual listed securities directly, there is limited availability of exchange-traded, index-referencing vehicles that provide a consolidated and methodology-driven exposure format through a single listed instrument.

The rationale for the Proposed Fund is therefore grounded in the objective of enhancing benchmark infrastructure and product depth within the Namibian capital markets ecosystem. A properly structured ETF referencing the NAX30 Index may support benchmarking, market accessibility, portfolio construction, and institutional engagement, while operating within a regulated framework and without displacing the role of the exchange or direct investment channels.

The Proposed Fund is not intended to replace direct investment in listed securities, nor to replicate exchange functions. Rather, it is intended to operate as a complementary financial product that translates a benchmark methodology into a regulated access vehicle.

## 7. The NAX30 Index

### 7.1 Definition

The NAX30 Index is a rules-based equity benchmark designed to measure the performance of a selected group of securities listed on the Namibia Securities Exchange.

The Index is intended to function as a benchmark reference measure. It is not, in itself, a fund, security, or regulated investment product. It does not represent ownership in the underlying securities and does not confer direct claims on any listed issuer.

### 7.2 Benchmark Role

The NAX30 Index is intended to serve several institutional functions. It is intended to operate as a market reference, a benchmarking layer for analysis and allocation, and a foundational reference for the development of benchmark-linked financial products.

In this sense, the Index is the common reference layer that may support more than one market expression while remaining conceptually and structurally distinct from those expressions.

### 7.3 Methodology Framework

The Index is intended to be constructed pursuant to a defined methodology. That methodology is expected to specify the eligible universe of securities, the constituent selection framework, the weighting logic, the rebalancing schedule, and the treatment of corporate actions and extraordinary events.

Constituent eligibility is expected to be based on objective factors that may include market capitalization, liquidity, listing status, and other methodology-defined criteria relevant to benchmark construction.

The weighting framework is expected to be rules-based and designed to balance representation and diversification, while remaining appropriate to the characteristics of the Namibian listed market.

Rebalancing is expected to occur periodically in accordance with the methodology, with provisions for scheduled reviews as well as defined treatment for extraordinary corporate actions, suspensions, delistings, mergers, rights issues, stock splits, and other events that may affect index continuity or integrity.

The detailed methodology is expected to be maintained in a separate methodology document and may be appended to future institutional or regulatory materials.

## 9. Investment Objective

The investment objective of the Proposed Fund is to track, as closely as reasonably practicable and before fees and expenses, the performance of the NAX30 Index.

The Proposed Fund is intended to provide a transparent and rules-based mechanism through which investors may obtain exposure to the benchmark performance of a defined basket of Namibian listed equities.

There can be no assurance that the Proposed Fund will achieve its stated objective. Tracking error may arise as a result of fees, transaction costs, liquidity conditions, implementation constraints, valuation issues, rebalancing mechanics, cash holdings, and other operational factors.

---

## 10. Investment Policy and Strategy

The Proposed Fund is expected to pursue a passive investment strategy designed to replicate, or closely approximate, the composition and performance characteristics of the NAX30 Index.

Under normal market conditions, the Proposed Fund is expected to invest primarily in the equity securities that form part of the Index. Depending on legal, operational, and liquidity considerations, replication may be achieved through either full replication or optimized sampling.

Under a full replication model, the Fund would seek to hold all constituent securities of the Index in weights that closely reflect the methodology. Under an optimized sampling approach, the Fund may hold a representative subset of constituents or adjust weights in a manner intended to approximate benchmark performance while accounting for liquidity or execution constraints.

The Proposed Fund may also hold cash or cash equivalents for liquidity management purposes, including in connection with subscriptions, redemptions, dividend flows, rebalancing activity, and settlement processes.

The treatment of illiquid securities, suspended securities, or securities subject to corporate action events is expected to be handled in accordance with the benchmark methodology, the fund operating model, and the policies of the appointed fund manager and administrator.

The Proposed Fund is not currently intended to be an actively managed strategy. Its role is not to outperform the benchmark, but to track it within the constraints of real-world fund implementation.

## 11. Benchmark Governance Framework

### 11.1 Governance Objective

The NAX30 Index is intended to operate under a formal governance framework designed to ensure methodological consistency, transparency, integrity, and appropriate separation from product-level commercial interests.

### 11.2 Methodology Oversight

A formal methodology document is expected to govern all key aspects of index construction and maintenance. The methodology is intended to define the rules for constituent eligibility, selection, weighting, rebalancing, corporate action treatment, and extraordinary events.

Methodology changes are expected to be governed by a defined process. Such changes would ordinarily require internal review, documentation, and, where appropriate, notice to relevant counterparties or users of the benchmark.

### 11.3 Index Committee

An Index Committee may be established to oversee benchmark governance. The purpose of the Index Committee would be to review methodology application, evaluate exceptional cases, consider proposed methodology changes, and support the integrity of benchmark maintenance.

The composition, appointment process, and decision-making procedures of the Index Committee are expected to be documented in a governance framework. Conflict-of-interest controls are expected to be implemented to ensure that benchmark governance remains independent of short-term product-level commercial considerations.

### 11.4 Cessation and Replacement

The benchmark governance framework is also expected to address the possibility of benchmark cessation, material methodology change, or replacement. In such cases, procedures would need to be defined for orderly transition, user notification, and product-level continuity planning.

## 12. Benchmark Licensing Architecture

The NAX30 Index is intended to be licensed to the Proposed Fund under a formal benchmark licensing agreement.

That licensing relationship is expected to define the rights of the ETF vehicle and its appointed manager to use the benchmark for fund operation, naming, branding, reference, and product documentation purposes.

The licensing architecture is also expected to address economic terms, which may include benchmark licensing fees or other commercial arrangements, together with protections designed to preserve the independence of benchmark governance.

A fundamental institutional principle of the proposed structure is that the Index Provider would not control day-to-day portfolio management or regulated fiduciary decisions of the ETF. The benchmark license is intended to provide benchmark usage rights, not operational control over the fund.

This separation is important because it preserves the benchmark as an independent reference layer while allowing the ETF to function as a regulated investment vehicle under licensed and supervised management.

## 13. Market Data and Valuation Framework

The operation of the Proposed Fund is expected to depend on reliable, timely, and properly sourced market data relating to the securities listed on the Namibia Securities Exchange.

The valuation of the Proposed Fund's portfolio is expected to be based primarily on official market prices, including exchange closing prices where available and appropriate. The detailed valuation policy would be determined by the appointed fund manager and administrator in accordance with applicable regulation and accepted valuation practices.

Where market prices are unavailable, stale, or unreliable, fair value methodologies may need to be applied under a documented valuation policy. Such circumstances may arise in connection with illiquid securities, suspended trading, market disruptions, or other valuation anomalies.

The benchmark calculation process and the fund NAV calculation process are expected to operate with coherent pricing hierarchies and documented rules in order to reduce unnecessary inconsistency between benchmark reference values and fund valuation.

The use of exchange data or other market data sources may be subject to licensing arrangements or permissions, and exchange engagement may therefore form an important part of launch readiness.

## 14. ETF Operating Mechanics

### 14.1 Fund Units

Units in the Proposed Fund are expected to represent proportional ownership interests in the net assets of the fund, subject to the final legal form and applicable law.

### 14.2 Creation and Redemption

The Proposed Fund is expected to create and redeem units in large blocks, typically through authorized participants or equivalent institutional counterparties. The final mechanics may involve cash creations and redemptions, in-kind creations and redemptions, or a combination thereof, depending on legal and operational design.

The final creation and redemption model will need to reflect the practical realities of the Namibian market, the underlying benchmark constituents, settlement processes, liquidity conditions, and the preferences of the appointed fund manager and service providers.

### 14.3 Secondary Market Trading

Units of the Proposed Fund are intended to be listed and traded on the Namibia Securities Exchange. Secondary market trading would permit investors to buy and sell units through the exchange, subject to market hours, broker access, order book depth, and exchange rules.

As with other exchange-traded products, the secondary market price of fund units may trade at a premium or discount to NAV depending on supply, demand, market liquidity, and the effectiveness of market-making and creation/redemption activity.

### 14.4 NAV and Indicative Pricing

Net Asset Value is expected to be calculated on a periodic basis, likely daily, subject to final operating design. Additional indicative pricing or iNAV-style mechanisms may be considered depending on exchange infrastructure, market data capability, and the preferences of the final fund manager.

## 19. Risk Considerations

The Proposed Fund, if established, would be subject to a range of risks that are relevant both at the benchmark level and at the fund implementation level.

Market risk would arise from fluctuations in the prices of the underlying listed securities. Given the characteristics of the Namibian listed market, the Proposed Fund may also be exposed to concentration risk, in which a relatively limited number of issuers or sectors may materially influence benchmark and fund performance.

Liquidity risk may be significant where one or more benchmark constituents trade infrequently or in limited volumes. This may affect replication accuracy, increase transaction costs, or complicate rebalancing.

Tracking error risk may arise from fees, expenses, cash drag, valuation differences, liquidity constraints, implementation choices, sampling methods, or timing mismatches between benchmark maintenance and fund execution.

Benchmark methodology risk may arise if the methodology produces outcomes that do not align with market expectations or if methodology changes affect benchmark behavior over time.

Benchmark governance risk may arise if benchmark oversight is insufficiently robust, if conflicts are not properly managed, or if methodology changes are not governed appropriately.

Market data risk may arise where data permissions, data quality, valuation inputs, or exchange data dependencies affect benchmark or fund operations.

Premium and discount risk may arise because ETF units may trade above or below NAV in the secondary market depending on market conditions, liquidity, and creation/redemption effectiveness.

Operational risk may arise through failures or disruptions involving service providers, custody, administration, settlement, valuation, compliance, technology, or human processes.

Regulatory and legal risk may arise because the final classification, approval pathway, and compliance requirements of the Proposed Fund may evolve during legal and regulatory review. Tax treatment may also vary depending on structure and jurisdiction.

Launch risk is also material. There is no assurance that the Proposed Fund will proceed to establishment, obtain approvals, secure service providers, or reach viable scale.

Investors in any future established fund may lose part or all of their invested capital.

## 20. Relationship Between the NAX30 ETF and the NAX30 Token

### 20.1 Core Principle

The relationship between the [NAX30 ETF](https://nax30.com) and any [NAX30 token](https://nax30.io) traded on a centralized exchange (CEX) must be understood with precision.

They are not the same instrument. They do not occupy the same regulatory category. They do not represent interchangeable claims. They are separate market expressions that may reference the same underlying benchmark.

The common reference layer is the NAX30 Index.

### 20.2 Structural Relationship

The correct institutional structure is as follows:

```text
                    NAX30 Index (Benchmark)
                              |
          +-------------------+-------------------+
          |                                       |
          |                                       |
   NAX30 ETF (NSX)                        NAX30 Token (CEX)
   Regulated financial product            Digital market instrument
   Exchange-traded fund                   Centralized exchange traded
   Institutionally governed               Market-driven
   Intended to track benchmark            May reference benchmark
   Separate legal structure               Separate legal/economic profile
```

### 20.3 NAX30 ETF

The NAX30 ETF is intended to be a regulated financial product structured as an exchange-traded fund (ETF). It is expected to operate within a formal legal, regulatory, and institutional framework and to be managed by an appropriately licensed fund manager in accordance with applicable Namibian law and supervisory requirements.

The ETF is intended to be listed on the Namibia Securities Exchange (NSX), subject to regulatory approval, exchange approval, satisfaction of listing requirements, and completion of the necessary structuring, governance, and service-provider arrangements. Its purpose is to provide investors with a regulated, benchmark-linked investment vehicle that seeks to track the performance of the NAX30 Index.

The ETF is not intended to be a discretionary or actively managed product. Its investment objective is expected to be benchmark-tracking in nature, with portfolio construction and fund operations designed to replicate, or closely approximate, the methodology-defined exposure represented by the NAX30 Index.

The ETF is expected to function as an institutional-grade investment product. As such, it would be subject to formal requirements relating to governance, disclosure, custody, administration, valuation, compliance, and, if established, ongoing regulatory oversight.

The ETF is also expected to be distinct from the benchmark itself. The benchmark serves as the reference layer, while the ETF would serve as the regulated investment wrapper referencing that benchmark. The ETF would therefore not be the benchmark, and the benchmark would not itself be the ETF.

### 20.4 NAX30 Token

Any NAX30 token traded on a centralized exchange is a separate digital market instrument and must not be confused with the proposed ETF.

A tokenized or digitally traded expression of NAX30 may reference the same underlying benchmark, but it does not thereby become a regulated fund, a unit of the ETF, or a legally equivalent instrument. Its market behavior, legal classification, exchange environment, counterparties, liquidity dynamics, and investor protections may differ materially from those applicable to a regulated ETF.

The NAX30 token, if traded on a centralized exchange, would generally be expected to function as a distinct market instrument whose price may be influenced by market demand, trading activity, exchange conditions, and broader digital asset market behavior. Even where the token references the benchmark, its trading environment is separate from that of the proposed ETF and from that of the Namibia Securities Exchange.

The token is not intended, for purposes of this whitepaper, to represent ownership in the ETF, beneficial interest in the ETF, a claim on ETF assets, or a right to receive ETF units. It is not intended to be redeemable into ETF units or into the underlying basket of securities held or referenced by the ETF.

Similarly, the ETF is not intended to derive its legal standing, valuation methodology, or regulatory status from any token market. The ETF must be understood as a separate regulated financial product, while the token must be understood as a separate digital market instrument.

### 20.5 Legal and Institutional Separation

The distinction between the NAX30 ETF and any NAX30 token is fundamental and must be maintained with precision.

The existence of a common benchmark reference does not create common legal character, common asset backing, common investor rights, or common market structure. The ETF and the token may both reference the NAX30 Index, but they remain separate instruments operating in separate legal, regulatory, operational, and market environments.

The ETF is intended to exist within a regulated fund framework. It is expected to be subject to licensing, regulatory supervision, formal disclosure standards, institutional governance, and service-provider oversight. The token, by contrast, would trade in a different market environment and would not, by virtue of referencing the benchmark, become part of the ETF structure or acquire the characteristics of a regulated fund.

The token does not constitute a class of ETF units, a depositary receipt over ETF units, a synthetic legal extension of the ETF, or a redeemable claim over the ETF or its assets. The ETF does not constitute a wrapper over the token, a listed representation of the token, or a mechanism for validating or supporting the token market.

The benchmark is therefore the only common layer between the two. Beyond that shared reference layer, the ETF and the token must be treated as separate instruments with separate legal identities, separate risk profiles, separate market infrastructures, and separate disclosure obligations.

Neither instrument confers any legal claim, beneficial interest, or entitlement in the other, and no mechanism exists for conversion, redemption, or exchange between the NAX30 ETF and any NAX30 token.

### 20.6 Clear Institutional Positioning

The correct institutional position is that the NAX30 ETF is the proposed regulated investment product, while any NAX30 token is a separate digital market instrument.

This distinction should be maintained consistently across whitepapers, disclosures, presentations, institutional outreach, website materials, regulatory discussions, and exchange engagement.

For the avoidance of doubt, the benchmark should be described as the common reference layer, the ETF should be described as the regulated financial product, and the token should be described only as a separate market instrument that may reference the same benchmark without forming part of the ETF structure.

This distinction is not merely semantic. It is necessary to preserve legal clarity, support sound product classification, avoid investor misunderstanding, and protect the integrity of any eventual regulatory and institutional engagement concerning the ETF.

## 26. Closing Statement

The NAX30 ETF is being developed as a proposed institutional financial product designed to translate the NAX30 benchmark into a regulated exchange-traded fund structure.

The benchmark and the ETF must be understood as distinct but related layers. The benchmark serves as the reference foundation. The ETF is the proposed regulated wrapper intended to track that reference foundation through a formal investment product structure.

Any separate digital or tokenized market expression of NAX30 must likewise be understood as a distinct instrument whose relationship to the ETF is limited to a shared benchmark reference and not to shared ownership, backing, redeemability, or legal character.

The institutional credibility of the Proposed Fund depends on preserving these distinctions while building an appropriate governance, regulatory, legal, and operational framework around both the benchmark and the ETF structure.

For that reason, the NAX30 ETF should be evaluated not as a tokenized product, not as a benchmark substitute, and not as a speculative digital extension, but as a proposed regulated financial product whose success depends on benchmark integrity, sound structuring, institutional partnerships, regulatory discipline, and execution quality.
